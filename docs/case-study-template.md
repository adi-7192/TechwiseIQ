# Case Study Template + "New but Expert" Playbook

For the `/work` pages. Fill one per real project (2 websites + 1–2 apps at launch). **No fake projects, ever** — Dubai's market is small, people verify, and one discovered fake ends the "we know what we're doing" positioning permanently.

## Why depth beats volume

Two case studies written like engineering post-mortems signal more expertise than twelve thumbnail screenshots. The reader should finish thinking "they understood the problem" — not "they made something pretty."

## Template (per project)

```markdown
# [Project name] — [one-line outcome]
<!-- e.g., "Al Noor Trading — a 6-week replatform that doubled inquiry volume" -->

## Snapshot
- Client: [name, or "Confidential — {industry}" if no permission]
- Industry / location:
- Service: Web / Software / AI Automation
- Timeline: [real duration]
- Stack: [technologies — engineers and technical buyers read this]
- Live: [URL if public]

## The problem (2–3 sentences)
What was broken/missing in business terms. Their situation before us.
Write from the client's POV, not ours.

## Constraints (1–2 sentences — this is where expertise shows)
Budget, deadline, legacy system, content gaps. Honest constraints make
the result credible.

## What we did
3–5 short paragraphs or bullets. Decisions, not features:
"We chose X over Y because Z." Include one thing that went wrong and
how it was handled — this builds more trust than a flawless narrative.

## The result
Real, specific, verifiable:
- Quantitative if possible (load time, conversion, hours saved, revenue)
- Qualitative if not (client quote WITH permission, or describe the
  operational change)
- NEVER invent numbers. "We don't have analytics access post-handover"
  is an acceptable sentence.

## Visuals
3–6 real screenshots, treated per design system (Kinetic: 3px ink
border + hard offset shadow; full color is fine — no filters required).
Before/after if it flatters the work.
```

## Honest ways to add weight while the list is short

- **"From the lab"** — self-initiated builds and demos, clearly labeled as internal projects. Shows range without deception. (An impressive demo you built for yourselves is still proof of capability.)
- **The website itself** — write a case study about building your own site: stack, performance numbers, design decisions. Recursive, honest, and exactly what a buyer of websites wants to read.
- **Teardowns** — short articles analyzing/improving real public sites (respectfully). Demonstrates judgment, feeds the Phase 2 blog, and gives sales conversations material.
